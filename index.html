Representing speech through autoregressive prediction of cochlear tokens

Anonymous Sumbission

We introduce a biologically-inspired model for encoding speech, leveraging a two-stage framework inspired by the human auditory processing hierarchy. The first stage transforms a raw audio waveform into a time-frequency representation based on the human cochlea, from which we read out discrete units which we denote as cochlear tokens. The second stage learns a simple, yet powerful, autoregressive sequence model over the cochlear tokens. We demonstrate that our model, AuriStream, learns meaningful representations of phonemes and word identities, and state-of-the-art representations of lexical semantics. In addition, AuriStream shows competitive performance on a diverse set of downstream speech tasks from the SUPERB benchmark. Complementing AuriStream's strong representational capabilties, we demonstrate its ability to generate continuations of audio at various temporal scales, which can be visualized in a spectrogram space, and decoded back into audio, to provide insights into the model's predictions.

We provide additional speech continuations generated by our model (AuriStream-large) to showcase its generation capabilities. We present speech continuations under various seeds for several samples from the LibriSpeech test set and TIMIT test set (not present in the training data). The speech continuations were generated by providing the model the beginning of an audio clip (2.5s), and having it predict the subsequent 2.5s.
